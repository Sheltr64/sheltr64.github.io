Why I'm introducing factor investing to my portfolio

I’ve always had a soft spot for value investing, looking at strong price-to-book and earnings multiples. However, I’ve never really given much thought to market cap size. I had always assumed that the small- to medium-cap universe was filled with a bunch of penny-stock gambling degenerates, so I stayed clear of that area, with my prejudices being proven again and again as large-cap growth stocks shattered records and put markets at all-time highs.

As I became more educated, I learned about the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), which is used to estimate a stock's long-term average return. This is great, right? Well, kinda. You see, the CAPM only explains ~60% of your actual return. This is where the Fama-French Three-factor model comes into play.

This is the CAPM

This is the Fama-French

Ignoring the alpha (I’m not even sure how to describe that god-forsaken concept), the Fama-French model is basically the CAPM with two new bells and whistles: Small Minus Big (SMB, derived from market cap) and High Minus Low (HML, derived from book-to-market ratio).

The Fama-French model explains ~90% of a stock's expected returns, meaning that by using this model, you can more accurately find a stock that has higher expected returns. The model argues that both small-cap and value stocks provide higher returns over the long term. By using these “factors,” I can create a portfolio that aligns with my goal of maximizing returns for a retirement portfolio.

For the ETFs I chose, my current weighting looks something like this:

  • 40% FXAIX (Fidelity 500 Index Fund)
  • 30% VXUS (Vanguard Total International Stock ETF)
  • 20% DFSV (Dimensional US Small Cap Value ETF)
  • 10% DISV (Dimensional International Small Cap Value ETF)

A portfolio this volatile requires an immense degree of patience (which I believe I can stomach) and is NOT for the faint of heart.
